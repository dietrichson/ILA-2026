In the months since Charlie Kirk was shot dead at a Turning Point USA event at Utah Valley University last September, his face has been grafted onto Ice Spice, Hitler, Taylor Swift, Bart Simpson, Drake, and the Joker. An AI-generated gospel-trap track titled "We Are Charlie Kirk" circulates alongside reams of nonsense vocabulary like "lowkirkentologicalowstate," half-syllables that read like the residue of a language model talking in its sleep. The images arrive faster than they can be parsed: Kirk as cartoon villain, Kirk as pop diva, Kirk as Saturday-morning sidekick, Kirk crooning in a voice that has been stitched together from a thousand sermons he never gave. Some of the pictures are crude composites, the seams visible where one face has been pasted over another; others are uncannily smooth, the work of diffusion models that have learned to soften every edge into plausibility. Together they form a kind of weather system, a low pressure front of imagery that drifts across the timeline at all hours, occasionally lifting to reveal the killing it formed around. The New Yorker article examines this phenomenon, which it calls "Kirkslop," and argues that what began as a nihilistic, dark-comic response to a public assassination has since revealed something more ominous. The same AI-driven memetic machinery that turned a dead activist into a shapeshifting digital mascot is now being weaponized by state actors as geopolitical propaganda. The line between a 4chan in-joke and a foreign-ministry communiqué, the piece suggests, has grown disconcertingly thin, and the speed at which an image can travel from one register to the other is now measured not in news cycles but in scrolls.

The article opens with Akhbar Enfejari, or Explosive News, a pro-Iran YouTube outfit whose AI-generated Lego-themed propaganda videos went viral in the wake of the U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iran. The aesthetic is jarring — toy-box animations selling a war narrative, plastic minifigures pantomiming missile strikes, the cheerful primary palette of a children's playroom recruited to dramatize the destruction of cities — but the form is familiar. The Trump administration helped normalize this register on TikTok and X by splicing bombing footage with pop-culture clips, modeling for the world a style of meme warfare in which atrocity and entertainment share a single feed, scrolled past with the same thumb. What Akhbar Enfejari understood, and what its imitators across the political spectrum have understood since, is that the grammar of the meme is now indistinguishable from the grammar of the press release. Both depend on compression, on the visual one-liner, on the assumption that the viewer has roughly two seconds in which to be persuaded of something before the next image takes its place. Slopaganda, the piece suggests, is a vernacular of statecraft, and the audience for it has been trained, over years of doomscrolling, to accept that the most serious things in the world will arrive in the costume of a joke. The decorum that once separated a foreign ministry from a meme account has been dissolved not by any single act of transgression but by the gradual normalization of a shared format. When the bomb and the bit share an aspect ratio, the difference between them becomes a matter of caption rather than substance. The Lego war footage is instructive precisely because it is so ostentatiously childish; its silliness is not a failure of the propaganda but the mechanism of it, the means by which an otherwise unspeakable image of violence is licensed to enter the feed. To watch a Lego missile demolish a Lego skyline is to be granted permission, momentarily, not to think about the real one. That permission, the article suggests, is what state communicators have learned to manufacture at scale.

Kirk's death supplied that vernacular with a perfect engine. Witnessed live by thousands at the event and by millions more on social media, the killing was instantaneously absorbed into the content economy, transmuted from event into feed before the news could even settle into a chyron. The footage existed in dozens of versions within minutes, slowed down, sped up, reframed, soundtracked, captioned in a hundred competing keys, and the algorithms that govern attention rewarded each new permutation with a fresh wave of distribution. The MAGA-sphere moved quickly to anoint Kirk as a martyr, and the consequences for those who criticized him — Jimmy Kimmel, educators, a Toledo firefighter, airline employees — became part of the story, a public ritual of enforcement in which transgression was named, surfaced, and punished in full view of an audience that had been invited to witness the punishment as a kind of secondary entertainment. Each suspension, each firing, each apology functioned as its own miniature spectacle, looping in the timeline alongside the slop, generating its own round of reaction videos and quote-tweets and stitched commentaries. The grief, the outrage, the score-settling, and the slapstick remixes all moved through the same channels at the same time, frequently within the same minute, frequently within the same screen. But the memes themselves, the article notes, quickly outran any single political project. Don Caldwell, the editor-in-chief of Know Your Meme, observes that the imagery escaped ideology almost on contact, propagating because it was uncanny and shareable rather than because it was right or left. A face-swap does not care who is making it or why; it answers only to the logic of recognition and surprise, the same logic that has governed visual humor since the first photocopied office gag. Yvette Granata, a media-art professor at the University of Michigan, frames the resulting flood as "playing fiddle while the Titanic sinks" — dark comedy metabolizing tragedy in real time, audiences laughing at the edge of something they cannot otherwise process, the laugh itself a kind of involuntary digestion. The joke is not separate from the grief; the joke is the only available vessel for it, the only form supple enough to hold a public killing without shattering. To laugh at Kirkslop is not necessarily to celebrate Kirk's death, the article implies, nor to mourn it; it is to acknowledge, however obliquely, that the feed has no other language for what just happened.

Kirk himself, the piece argues, was uniquely suited to this afterlife. He had spent his career saturating the internet — a three-hour daily show, the "Surrounded" YouTube debate series, an endless stream of viral campus confrontations — and the sheer volume of his output meant that his image, his voice, his cadences, his catchphrases were already lying around the digital commons in industrial quantities, ready to be scraped, recombined, and reissued. By the time of his death, there was effectively no posture, no expression, and no vocal inflection of his that did not exist somewhere on a server, available for training. He was malleable in life, performing by turns as troll, scholar, debater, and pundit, switching registers in a single broadcast and rarely settling into one stable persona. His positions on race, transgender identity, COVID, the 2020 election, the "great replacement," and his remark that gun deaths were "worth it" gave detractors plenty to seize on, a catalogue of provocations dense enough to sustain an entire counter-economy of clips and rebuttals. Yet his polymorphous public persona also made him strangely available to remix, neither fixed enough to resist transformation nor coherent enough to discipline it. The Kirk who appeared in one video as a sober debater of constitutional principles and in the next as a culture-war provocateur could just as easily appear, in the wake of his killing, as a cartoon character or a pop singer; each iteration could plausibly claim continuity with some earlier performance. He was, in effect, pre-memed: a figure whose image had been so thoroughly distributed and so thoroughly contested in life that, in death, the machinery had no work left to do but iterate. The remix did not have to invent him; it only had to keep him in motion.

The argumentative arc closes by linking these threads. Kirkification began as disenchanted play, an internet shrugging at horror, a kind of nervous improvisation in the face of a killing too public to ignore and too disorienting to address directly. But the tools that powered it — face-swaps, AI music, Lego-rendered war footage — are the same tools now driving state propaganda from Tehran to Washington, the same models, the same pipelines, the same compressed, frictionless aesthetics of recombination. What looks like teenagers cackling in a group chat and what looks like a foreign government waging an information campaign turn out, on inspection, to be operating from the same toolkit, often with the same off-the-shelf software, sometimes literally trained on each other's outputs. The porousness is the point, and the porousness is what makes the phenomenon resistant to ordinary forms of media critique; one cannot cleanly denounce slopaganda without also denouncing the joke, and one cannot defend the joke without inadvertently defending its weaponized cousin. The same shrug that greets a Kirk face-swap is the shrug that greets the Lego bombing run, and the shrug, repeated often enough, becomes a posture, and the posture, scaled across a population, becomes a politics. Kirk, the article concludes, will be remembered "as a meme so moldable it can fit any agenda, all the time" — a fitting epitaph for a figure who lived inside the feed, and a useful warning about a media environment in which the distinction between mourning, mockery, and mobilization has, perhaps permanently, collapsed into a single endlessly scrolling stream.
