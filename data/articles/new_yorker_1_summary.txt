In the months since Charlie Kirk was shot dead at a Turning Point USA event at Utah Valley University last September, his face has been grafted onto Ice Spice, Hitler, Taylor Swift, Bart Simpson, Drake, and the Joker. An AI-generated gospel-trap track titled "We Are Charlie Kirk" circulates alongside reams of nonsense vocabulary like "lowkirkentologicalowstate." The New Yorker article examines this phenomenon, which it calls "Kirkslop," and argues that what began as a nihilistic, dark-comic response to a public assassination has since revealed something more ominous: the same AI-driven memetic machinery is now being weaponized by state actors as geopolitical propaganda.

The article opens with Akhbar Enfejari, or Explosive News, a pro-Iran YouTube outfit whose AI-generated Lego-themed propaganda videos went viral in the wake of the U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iran. The aesthetic is jarring — toy-box animations selling a war narrative — but the form is familiar. The Trump administration helped normalize this register on TikTok and X by splicing bombing footage with pop-culture clips, modeling for the world a style of meme warfare in which atrocity and entertainment share a single feed. Slopaganda, the piece suggests, is now a vernacular of statecraft.

Kirk's death supplied that vernacular with a perfect engine. Witnessed live by thousands at the event and by millions more on social media, the killing was instantaneously absorbed into the content economy. The MAGA-sphere moved quickly to anoint Kirk as a martyr, and the consequences for those who criticized him — Jimmy Kimmel, educators, a Toledo firefighter, airline employees — became part of the story, a public ritual of enforcement. But the memes themselves, the article notes, quickly outran any single political project. Don Caldwell, the editor-in-chief of Know Your Meme, observes that the imagery escaped ideology almost on contact, propagating because it was uncanny and shareable rather than because it was right or left. Yvette Granata, a media-art professor at the University of Michigan, frames the resulting flood as "playing fiddle while the Titanic sinks" — dark comedy metabolizing tragedy in real time, audiences laughing at the edge of something they cannot otherwise process.

Kirk himself, the piece argues, was uniquely suited to this afterlife. He had spent his career saturating the internet — a three-hour daily show, the "Surrounded" YouTube debate series, an endless stream of viral campus confrontations — and he was malleable in life, performing by turns as troll, scholar, debater, and pundit. His positions on race, transgender identity, COVID, the 2020 election, the "great replacement," and his remark that gun deaths were "worth it" gave detractors plenty to seize on, but his polymorphous public persona also made him strangely available to remix. He was, in effect, pre-memed.

The argumentative arc closes by linking these threads. Kirkification began as disenchanted play, an internet shrugging at horror, but the tools that powered it — face-swaps, AI music, Lego-rendered war footage — are the same tools now driving state propaganda from Tehran to Washington. Kirk, the article concludes, will be remembered "as a meme so moldable it can fit any agenda, all the time."
