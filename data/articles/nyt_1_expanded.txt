On a weekday morning in Downtown Brooklyn, the small wedge of land that wraps around Borough Hall hums with the routines of a working civic district. Lawyers cut across the pavers with rolling cases. Office workers from the towers along Adams Street drift toward coffee carts. A handful of parents, increasingly visible in a neighborhood that has filled with apartment buildings over the past two decades, push strollers along the borders of Columbus Park, the green spine that runs in front of the borough's seat of government. And tucked off to one side, walled in by a rim of cars whose presence has become so normal it barely registers, sits a parking lot that, on paper, does not exist.

That paper, kept in Borough Hall, is a 1945 map with cloth pages, and it is at the center of a dispute that has flared up, gone dormant and flared up again for more than half a century. According to the map, the asphalt where roughly 50 State Supreme Court judges leave their cars each day is parkland, part of a 7.9-acre parcel that includes Borough Hall itself. According to the judges, it is something closer to an extension of the courthouse next door, a security perimeter that cannot be surrendered without putting the people who hand down rulings on the borough's most serious cases at risk. The argument over which of those descriptions should prevail has now reached a new mayor, a new corporation counsel and a new round of meetings, with both sides as dug in as they have been at any point in the dispute's long life.

The latest push to convert the lot back to green space is being led by two Brooklyn Democrats: Antonio Reynoso, the borough president, and Lincoln Restler, the City Council member whose district includes the courthouse complex. They are seeking to enlist Mayor Zohran Mamdani, whose administration is still settling into City Hall, in a fight their predecessors have variously taken up and set aside since the 1980s. Reynoso has gone further than simply asking for the lot back. He has offered a redesign vision for the site that imagines a lawn, a kiosk, a public restroom, plantings and walking paths, the sort of modest but deliberately useful amenities that have become standard in city parks reclaimed from other uses. He has also been waving the 1945 map, treating it as both a legal artifact and a rhetorical one.

The case for converting the lot rests in part on a demographic argument that has only grown more pointed with time. In 2004, the city rezoned Downtown Brooklyn to permit dense residential development, and over the following two decades the neighborhood transformed from a daytime office district into one of the fastest-growing residential quarters in the city. Apartment towers rose along Flatbush Avenue Extension, along Schermerhorn Street and along the side streets that feed into the courthouse cluster. With the new residents came a familiar New York problem: not enough green space to absorb them. Columbus Park, never large to begin with, became one of the few outdoor places in the immediate area where children could run and adults could sit. The fenced-off section behind the courthouse, in this telling, is not just a historical anomaly but an active subtraction from a neighborhood that needs every square foot it can get.

The judges see the same patch of asphalt very differently. Represented by the Board of Supreme Court Justices of Kings County, they argue that the lot is essential to their security, and they have framed the argument in terms that have become harder to dismiss in recent years. Threats against members of the judiciary have risen sharply, with the Department of Homeland Security warning of a notable spike last year, and the courthouse on Adams Street is no exception to the broader climate. Roughly 50 State Supreme Court justices work in the building, presiding over felony trials, civil disputes and family matters that routinely produce aggrieved parties. Abraham Gerges, a retired administrative judge who has defended the parking arrangement, has spoken about the kinds of threats that come with the job, recalling that he once received round-the-clock protection after an inmate tried to engage what he described as a "witch doctor" to place a hex on him. The anecdote, almost folkloric in its particulars, is meant to convey a more serious point: that the people who decide these cases live with a level of personal risk that most courthouse visitors never see.

Spokesmen for the courts have been similarly unyielding. Al Baker and Justin Barry, of the Office of Court Administration, have maintained that no alternative arrangement provides adequate security, a position that has effectively been the official line of the judiciary throughout the dispute. The lot, in their view, is not a perk. It is infrastructure.

The history of how the asphalt came to be there in the first place reads, in retrospect, like a small comedy of municipal planning. In 1958, a Daily News article mocked the construction of the new courthouse without provision for parking, leaving Robert Moses, then the city's parks commissioner and the most powerful unelected figure in the metropolitan area, flummoxed. By the following year, the city had quietly allowed the judges to park in Columbus Park, and a contemporaneous newspaper account described the site as exclusively reserved for the 18 justices then sitting in the building. What began as a stopgap hardened into custom. The lot, never legally redesignated, simply remained.

There have been moments over the decades when it appeared the arrangement might end. In 1999, an administrative judge promised to relocate the cars to a new garage on Jay Street. The promise was not kept. The judges did, however, give up 16 spaces on what had been a segment of Fulton Street, a partial concession that critics regard as evidence that the lot's footprint has always been negotiable when the political will exists.

Critics of the current arrangement argue that the alternatives are not theoretical but abundant. An April 2025 email from the Brooklyn transportation commissioner identified up to 46 on-street parking spots in the immediate vicinity, a number that could be expanded to as many as 160 with modest reorganization. Brooklyn Law School, whose Feil Hall garage sits within walking distance, has offered space. The Marriott Hotel and the adjacent office building include a 1,000-car garage that is already used by, among others, the Secret Service and the Brooklyn district attorney's office, two organizations whose own security requirements are not trivial. Reynoso, in one of the more colorful suggestions to surface during this round of negotiations, has even floated a valet service for the judges, a proposal that doubles as a quiet rebuttal to the security argument: if presidential protective details and the borough's chief prosecutor can manage with a shared commercial garage, the reasoning goes, so can the bench.

Opponents of the lot have also seized on a smaller, more visual irony. A vehicle in the lot bears a visible license plate reading "Supreme Court 76," a marker that, they argue, broadcasts the very information the security argument is supposed to conceal. If the point of the lot is to keep the judges' arrivals and departures invisible to anyone who might wish them harm, the critics ask, why advertise the affiliation on the bumper.

The dispute reached a new pitch last summer, after the city secured $21 million to remake Columbus Park with a playground, a skatepark and additional trees, an investment scaled to the neighborhood's transformed demographics. The judges responded by suing the city and the previous mayor, Eric Adams, citing a 2008 agreement that they said required their consent before any removal of their parking. Randy Mastro, who served as Adams's first deputy mayor, dismissed the suit as "frivolous," and the case was dropped after he assured the judges' lawyer that the plans were, for the moment, theoretical. The episode left both sides irritated and neither side persuaded.

Reynoso and Restler have not let the matter rest. They have continued pressing, looking for an opening with the new administration. Steven Banks, Mamdani's corporation counsel, recently met with Restler, who emerged from the conversation hopeful that the city might be willing to revisit the question with fresh eyes. The judges, for their part, appear unmoved. Their message to the politicians has been blunt: look elsewhere.

What is at stake in this small parcel goes beyond the immediate question of whether a few dozen cars stay or go. The dispute has become a test case for how a city should weigh the operational needs of its institutions against the everyday demands of the neighborhoods that surround them, and for how seriously decades-old maps and decades-old promises should be taken when the city around them has changed. Downtown Brooklyn in 2026 is not the Downtown Brooklyn of 1959, when the courthouse went up without parking and the city improvised a solution. The 18 justices have become 50. The office district has become a residential one. The map in Borough Hall, with its cloth pages and its quiet insistence that the lot is parkland, has acquired a new constituency in the strollers and skateboards now circling its edges. Whether that constituency or the one inside the courthouse will prevail is, for the moment, a question the new mayor will be asked to answer.
