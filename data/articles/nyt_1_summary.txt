A decades-old dispute over a parking lot in Downtown Brooklyn has flared up again, pitting state judges against parks advocates and elected officials. For more than 60 years, judges presiding at the neighboring courthouse on Adams Street have used a section of Columbus Park, mapped as parkland, as a private parking lot. For roughly half that time, public officials, neighborhood activists, and parents have argued the land should be returned to park use. The contested area sits within a 7.9-acre parcel that includes Borough Hall.

The renewed fight is being led by two Brooklyn Democrats, Borough President Antonio Reynoso and City Councilman Lincoln Restler, who are seeking to enlist the new mayor, Zohran Mamdani. Reynoso has presented a redesign vision featuring a lawn, kiosk, restroom, plantings, and paths, and points to a 1945 cloth-paged map in Borough Hall confirming the lot's status as parkland. A 2004 rezoning of Downtown Brooklyn intensified the area's shortage of green space, strengthening the case for conversion.

The judges, represented by the Board of Supreme Court Justices of Kings County, argue that the lot is essential to their security. Roughly 50 State Supreme Court judges work in the courthouse, and threats against the judiciary have risen sharply, with the Department of Homeland Security warning of a spike last year. Retired administrative judge Abraham Gerges defended the lot, recalling that he once received round-the-clock protection after an inmate sought a "witch doctor" to place a hex on him. Spokesman Al Baker and Justin Barry of the Office of Court Administration maintain that no alternative arrangement provides adequate security.

The history of the dispute stretches back to 1958, when a Daily News article mocked the construction of the courthouse without parking, leaving then-parks commissioner Robert Moses flummoxed. By 1959 the city allowed judges to park in Columbus Park, with a contemporary newspaper describing the site as exclusively for the 18 justices. In 1999 an administrative judge promised to move the cars to a new garage on Jay Street, a commitment that was not fulfilled, though judges gave up 16 spaces on a former segment of Fulton Street.

Critics argue ample alternatives exist. An April 2025 email from the Brooklyn transportation commissioner identified up to 46 on-street spots nearby, expandable to 160. Brooklyn Law School's Feil Hall garage has been offered, and the Marriott Hotel and adjacent office building include a 1,000-car garage already used by the Secret Service and the district attorney's office. Reynoso even floated a valet service. Opponents also note the irony of a vehicle in the lot bearing a visible "Supreme Court 76" license plate, which they say undermines security claims.

Last summer, after the city secured $21 million to remake Columbus Park with a playground, skatepark, and trees, the judges sued the city and then-Mayor Eric Adams, citing a 2008 agreement requiring their consent before removal. Adams's first deputy mayor, Randy Mastro, called the suit "frivolous," and it was dropped after he assured the judges' lawyer the plans were theoretical. Reynoso and Restler have continued pressing. Mamdani's corporation counsel, Steven Banks, recently met with Restler, who left hopeful, while the judges remain unmoved, telling the politicians to look elsewhere.
