In the warm, salt-bright waters off La Ventana, a small coastal town on Mexico's Baja California peninsula, a captain named Claudio Rios listens to the chatter on his marine radio and watches the horizon for a black dorsal fin slicing through the chop. Rios, 41, is one of a growing number of small-boat operators in the Sea of Cortez who make their living from a strange and increasingly popular form of ecotourism: dropping paying tourists, in wet suits, snorkels, and masks, into open ocean alongside wild killer whales. The orcas he tracks are familiar to him by name. There is Moctezuma, Cuitlahuac, and Tlaloc, resident males nicknamed for figures from Aztec history, animals that move through the gulf in search of Mobula rays, whales, dolphins, sharks, and bonefish, hunting with the methodical intelligence that has made their species the most formidable predators in the world's oceans.

According to an article published April 29, 2026, in The New York Times by the reporter Alexa Robles-Gil, La Ventana is one of only two places on the planet where the practice of swimming with wild killer whales is currently offered to the general public. The other is Skjervoy, a remote port town in Arctic Norway, where every winter from November through January, orcas pour into the fjords to gorge on the seasonal migrations of herring. In both locations, tourists drawn largely by social media imagery are paying to share the water, sometimes only a few feet away, with 20-foot apex predators. The article raises mounting concerns about the safety of both humans and whales, as crowds expand faster than the rules meant to govern them.

Killer whales are not, in the strict taxonomic sense, whales at all. They are the largest members of the dolphin family, intelligent, intensely social mammals that live in tight matrilineal pods, hunt cooperatively, and are found in every ocean on Earth, from the polar pack ice to the equator. For decades, most people who saw one in person did so through the thick acrylic of an aquarium tank. The cultural reckoning that followed the 2013 documentary Blackfish, which scrutinized the captive orca industry, helped shift public attitudes sharply against keeping the animals on display. Many of the same tourists who would not now buy a ticket to a marine park feel a powerful pull to encounter orcas instead in the wild, where the animals can hunt, breathe, and travel as they choose. The two villages now at the center of this niche tourism sit at the meeting point of that ethical migration and a much older one: the predictable seasonal movement of fish that makes the orcas appear so reliably each year.

In La Ventana, the industry developed for years in what was effectively a legal gray zone. Mexico introduced a permitting system for tour operators only last year, after a long stretch in which boats simply went out, found whales, and put swimmers in the water. Even with the new rules, Rios estimates that during the May-June peak, when the resident males are reliably present, roughly 100 boats can crowd the waters around a single pod. Not all of them carry permits. Tours run about $100 per person, an accessible price by international ecotourism standards, and the income has become significant enough that some local families have shifted away from night fishing toward marine tourism. Boats that once worked dark hours under the stars now circle through the day in pursuit of fins.

That economic pivot is part of what Georgina Saad, a marine biologist with Orcas Pacifico Mexicano, describes as a community-driven evolution of the industry. Saad has noted that scientists working in the gulf initially hoped the operation could remain limited to surface whale watching, with tourists observing the animals from boats but not entering the water. The local community pushed back, demanding the more dramatic, more lucrative in-water encounters that the international market wanted. The result is the current arrangement, in which dozens of operators of varying experience and training maneuver around the same small group of habituated whales, each carrying clients hoping for the photograph that will, in turn, draw the next wave of clients.

Across the Atlantic and far to the north, in the deep, dark fjords of Skjervoy, the same dynamic plays out in colder water and at much higher prices. Tours there can cost $400. There are, at present, no rules comparable to Mexico's permitting system. The Norwegian operations work in the polar twilight of the Arctic winter, when the sun barely clears the horizon and the herring boil up out of the depths into the narrow, mountain-walled inlets, drawing the orcas in dense pursuit. To enter the water in those conditions, even in a thick wet suit, is to feel the cold settle hard against the chest, the breath shorten, and the world reduce to the sound of one's own snorkel and the slow, looming shapes of black-and-white bodies in the gray-green dim.

Eve Jourdain, 38, is the director of the Norwegian Orca Survey and one of the most prominent scientific voices in Skjervoy. She captains a research boat among the same pods the tour operators visit, but she will not enter the water with the orcas herself. Jourdain's team has tracked individual whales in the area since 2013, building one of the most detailed long-term records of the population. Her group is the first to measure cortisol, a hormone associated with stress, in orca blubber, comparing levels recorded during the busy tourist season against baselines collected during the pandemic-era lull, when boat traffic in the fjords largely vanished. The work is part of a broader effort to put hard numbers on what many on the water already suspect.

Other voices in Skjervoy have stepped back from the trade entirely. The photographer Krisztina Balotay, who once led snorkel tours in the area, stopped doing so after she watched single pods become ringed by more than 20 boats at a time. Underwater photographer Jorge Cervera Hauser, who has worked extensively with marine megafauna, has warned that the constant human pressure makes a serious accident eventually inevitable, even though no wild orca has ever been documented attacking a human being. The species' near-perfect record in the open ocean, despite their hunting power, is part of what reassures the tourists who line up for tickets. It is also, the experts caution, part of what makes complacency dangerous.

The scientific case for restraint extends well beyond orcas. The Times article cites a 2021 Colombian study showing that the noise of a single whale-watching boat could mask 63 percent of a humpback whale's song, drowning out the long, low communications by which the animals find mates and coordinate movement. Research in Washington State has linked vessel presence to endangered female orcas abandoning foraging behavior, with possible consequences for reproduction in an already fragile population. Some jurisdictions have begun to act on such findings. New Zealand prohibits swimming within 300 feet of orcas. British Columbia fines boats for close approaches. In La Ventana and Skjervoy, the equivalent buffers either do not exist or exist on paper without consistent enforcement.

A loose international community of researchers has been pressing for tighter rules. Elena Sasso of Sapienza University, Marten Bril of Whale2Sea, Jorge Urban of the Autonomous University of Baja California Sur, and Mario Pardo of CICESE have all flagged a yawning knowledge gap and weak enforcement around the in-water tours. The University of Bergen anthropologist Sadie Hale, who studies the human side of the trade, called the current scene a "Wild West" in a 2025 study. Urban has pointed to a particular legal quirk that may matter for the future of the industry: because orcas are technically dolphins, they fall outside some of the legal protections afforded to true whales, leaving open the possibility that similar tourism could expand into other dolphin species without triggering the same regulatory scrutiny.

Norway's government has indicated, at least, that it has heard the warnings. The Norwegian fisheries minister, Marianne Sivertsen Naess, has pledged that distance requirements for vessels and snorkelers will be in place by next season. Jourdain, who has watched the fjord traffic grow year over year while monitoring her cortisol samples and her photo-identification catalog, remains skeptical that meaningful change will arrive in time. She has told reporters she fears the threshold for action will be reached only after someone is seriously hurt.

That is the pattern Robles-Gil's article ultimately documents: an ecotourism boom outpacing the science meant to constrain it and the rules meant to channel it. The pull is genuine. People want to see these animals as they are, not as they have been kept, and the orcas, for their part, are capable of inspiring something close to awe in anyone who has slipped into the same water with them. But the economics of the encounter, the price of the ticket, the power of the social media post, and the seasonal predictability of the prey all combine to push more boats and more swimmers into smaller and smaller patches of ocean. In La Ventana, the captains coordinate by radio and try to keep the swarm under control. In Skjervoy, the lights of the boats glow against the long Arctic night. In both places, the orcas keep coming back to the fish, and the people keep coming back to the orcas, and the gap between what is happening and what anyone has agreed should happen continues to widen.
