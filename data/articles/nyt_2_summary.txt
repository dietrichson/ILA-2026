Alexa Robles-Gil's April 29, 2026 article in The New York Times examines the rapidly growing practice of swimming with wild killer whales, a rare form of ecotourism currently offered in only two places on the planet: La Ventana, a small coastal town on Mexico's Baja California peninsula, and Skjervoy, in Arctic Norway. In both locations, tourists in wet suits, snorkels, and masks slip into open ocean alongside 20-foot apex predators, drawn by social media imagery and a desire to see orcas in the wild rather than in captivity. The article raises mounting concerns about the safety of both humans and whales as crowds expand faster than regulation.

In La Ventana, Claudio Rios, a 41-year-old captain, monitors radio traffic and tries to coordinate boats around resident male orcas nicknamed for Aztec figures, including Moctezuma, Cuitlahuac, and Tlaloc. The orcas hunt Mobula rays, whales, dolphins, sharks, and bonefish in the Sea of Cortez. Mexico introduced a permitting system for tour operators last year after the industry long operated in a legal gray zone, but Rios estimates roughly 100 boats crowd the waters during the May-June peak, not all of them permitted. Tours cost about $100, and some families have shifted from night fishing to marine tourism. Marine biologist Georgina Saad of Orcas Pacifico Mexicano notes that scientists initially wanted only whale watching, but the local community pushed for in-water encounters.

In Skjervoy, where orcas follow herring migrations into the fjords from November through January, tours can cost $400 and there are no comparable rules. Eve Jourdain, 38, director of the Norwegian Orca Survey, captains a research boat but refuses to enter the water. Her team has tracked individual orcas since 2013 and is the first to measure cortisol in blubber, comparing tourist-season levels with pandemic-era baselines. Photographer Krisztina Balotay stopped leading snorkel tours because pods can be ringed by more than 20 boats. Underwater photographer Jorge Cervera Hauser warns that constant human pressure makes an accident inevitable, even though no wild orca has attacked a human.

The article cites scientific evidence of disturbance: a 2021 Colombian study showed a single whale-watching boat could mask 63 percent of a humpback's song, and Washington research linked vessel presence to endangered female orcas abandoning foraging, with possible reproductive consequences. New Zealand bans swimming within 300 feet of orcas, and British Columbia fines close approaches. Researchers Elena Sasso of Sapienza University, Marten Bril of Whale2Sea, Jorge Urban of the Autonomous University of Baja California Sur, Mario Pardo of CICESE, and University of Bergen anthropologist Sadie Hale, who called the scene a "Wild West" in a 2025 study, all describe a knowledge gap and weak enforcement. Urban notes that because orcas are technically dolphins, they lack the legal protection afforded to true whales, potentially exposing other species to similar tourism.

Norwegian fisheries minister Marianne Sivertsen Naess has pledged distance requirements for vessels and snorkelers next season, but Jourdain remains skeptical that meaningful change will arrive before someone is hurt.
