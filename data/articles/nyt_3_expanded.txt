For more than three decades, the Cascios occupied a singular place in the orbit around Michael Jackson. They were not employees, not collaborators, not hangers-on in the conventional Hollywood sense. They were, by their own repeated description, his "second family." When accusations of child molestation against the singer surfaced and resurfaced over the years, members of the family stepped forward, often unbidden, to vouch for him. They sat for television interviews. They appeared at memorials. They presented themselves to the world as living rebuttals to the worst things people had said about the most famous entertainer of his generation.

That carefully maintained public posture has now collapsed. The Cascio family is suing Jackson's estate, alleging in a federal lawsuit that the singer repeatedly sexually abused four of their five children over many years, beginning when the children were as young as 7. The suit, taken together with a recent interview given to The New York Times, recasts decades of public friendship as something the family now describes as a long, calculated pattern of grooming and abuse, sustained through proximity and protected by silence the family itself helped enforce.

The relationship traces back to the 1980s, when Jackson, then at the apex of a global fame few human beings have ever known, befriended Dominic Cascio, a Manhattan hotel manager. What began as an acquaintance in a hotel lobby ripened into something almost domestic. Over the next two decades, by the family's own account and by the lawsuit's narrative, Jackson dined regularly at the Cascios' home in New Jersey, brought the family to his Neverland Ranch in California, took them along on world tours and spent holidays in their company. They moved in and out of his life with a frequency more typical of relatives than of friends. Their children grew up around him. He, in turn, grew comfortable in their house, eventually staying there for months at a time, including a long residence in the period after Sept. 11, 2001.

The most public expression of that closeness came in December 2010, roughly a year and a half after Jackson's death from acute propofol intoxication had stunned the world. The parents and three of the Cascio siblings appeared on Oprah Winfrey's show, then still one of the most visible cultural platforms in the United States, a place where reputations were burnished and rehabilitated under the host's watchful eye. The Cascios used that platform to defend Jackson with unusual unanimity. They denied, in unison, that any "improprieties" had occurred during the long years of their friendship with him. They described the late singer as the target of false accusations, a man whose generosity and gentleness had been distorted by people seeking attention or money.

According to the lawsuit and the family members now speaking publicly, that defense was untrue. The four siblings participating in the suit say they were groomed from a young age to act as Jackson's "soldiers," recruited into a defensive posture before they were old enough to understand what they were defending or why. The grooming, they allege, was inseparable from the abuse itself.

Aldo Cascio, now 35, says Jackson began performing oral sex on him when he was about 7 years old, and that the abuse continued for years thereafter, occurring at Jackson's home, on family trips and on the singer's tours. Aldo says that in the days before Jackson's death in 2009, the singer used the code phrase "Disneyland" to request a sexual encounter, a phrase that, according to the suit, had become a familiar shorthand between them. Marie Nicole Porte, 37, says she was 12 years old when Jackson first abused her in her own family's home, the same home where, after Sept. 11, the singer would spend months as an extended guest. Dominic, the son who shares his father's name, now 39, says the abuse began for him around the age of 8 during a trip to Euro Disney. Eddie, 43, says Jackson first molested him during a 1993 trip to Tel Aviv, a trip undertaken while Jackson was facing an earlier molestation accusation that would later be resolved through a roughly $23 million settlement in 1994. A fifth sibling, Frank, also says he was abused. He is in arbitration with the estate and is unable to join the federal suit.

The four plaintiffs describe varying paths to disclosure. Some, the suit says, recognized at the time that what was happening was wrong, but felt unable to speak about it, bound by a sense of obligation, by the family's deep enmeshment with the singer, and by the basic asymmetry of power between a child and one of the most famous men alive. Others, the suit says, did not fully understand what had happened to them until much later, when fragments of memory were assembled into something coherent only by external prompting.

That prompting, for the Cascios, came in 2019, with the release of the HBO documentary "Leaving Neverland." The film, which centered on two other men's accounts of childhood abuse by Jackson, set off the most sustained cultural reckoning the singer had faced since his death, prompting radio stations to pull his songs, museums to reconsider tributes and a generation of fans to grapple with the gap between the music they loved and the allegations that had trailed its maker for decades. The Cascios watched it. According to the suit and the Times interview, Aldo disclosed to his siblings first, and the others followed in turn, each recognition of the past triggering the next.

What followed was not a lawsuit but a negotiation. Beginning in 2019, the Cascios met with representatives of Jackson's estate. By 2020, they had reached a settlement under which each plaintiff received approximately $690,000 annually for five years, an arrangement that totaled roughly $16 million across the family. The estate, in subsequent filings, has said it agreed to the settlement "reluctantly," characterizing the payments as a measure undertaken to spare Jackson's family further accusations the estate considered false rather than as any acknowledgment of wrongdoing.

Those payments ended in 2025. Once they did, the relationship between the family and the estate deteriorated. According to filings by the estate, a lawyer for Frank, the sibling now in arbitration, demanded an additional $213 million in 2024, threatening a public lawsuit if the demand was not met. The estate did not meet it. The federal suit followed.

Jackson and his estate have consistently denied all molestation allegations against him, in life and in death. Marty Singer, a lawyer for the estate, has characterized the new suit as a "desperate money grab" and a "forum-shopping" tactic, describing the Cascios as unreliable sources whose accounts have shifted over time. The estate has signaled that it intends to seek dismissal of the federal case or a stay of proceedings pending the outcome of arbitration, the legal forum in which Frank's claims continue to play out separately.

The suit arrives at a charged moment for Jackson's posthumous reputation. A biopic of the singer, produced in cooperation with his estate, is set to debut soon. The film, by the estate's own design, covers only events through 1988, ending its narrative before the first publicly known molestation accusations against Jackson surfaced. That timing, intentional or not, has placed the rollout of a sanctioned celebration of Jackson's early career in unavoidable proximity to the most damaging civil action ever brought against his estate.

The legal terrain ahead is difficult, as it generally is when civil litigants pursue claims against the estates of deceased public figures. The accused cannot testify on his own behalf, but neither can he be cross-examined. Decades-old allegations turn on memory, contemporaneous documentation that may or may not exist, and the credibility of witnesses whose accounts must be reconstructed from a vanished period. Estates of wealthy entertainers are typically well-resourced, durable legal entities, structured to defend the commercial value of a name and a body of work for decades after the artist's death, and Jackson's estate, which has earned billions through music rights, a Cirque du Soleil show and the sale of his publishing catalog, is among the most formidable such entities in the entertainment industry.

The Cascios' suit also lands in the broader conversation about delayed disclosure of childhood sexual abuse, a conversation that has been substantially reshaped over the past decade. Researchers and clinicians have long noted that survivors often disclose decades after the fact, if at all, and that disclosures are frequently triggered not by therapy but by external events: a news story, a documentary, the testimony of another survivor. The Cascios' account, in which the HBO film served as the catalyst that allowed siblings to name what had happened, fits a pattern familiar to those who have studied such cases, even as the estate disputes the underlying allegations.

What is not in dispute is that for decades the Cascios stood publicly with Jackson, and that they did so loudly. The same family that once sat on a daytime television couch and rejected the idea of any improprieties is now asking a federal court to find that those improprieties were systematic, prolonged and devastating. The estate, in turn, says the family's earlier defense was the truthful one, and that the current suit is driven by money rather than memory. Between those two accounts lies a record that a court will now be asked to weigh, against the long shadow of a man no longer present to answer for himself.
