The Cascio family, who for decades described themselves as Michael Jackson's "second family" and publicly defended him against accusations of child molestation, is now suing his estate, alleging that Jackson repeatedly sexually abused four of their five children over many years.

The relationship began in the 1980s, when Jackson befriended Dominic Cascio, a Manhattan hotel manager. Over the next two decades, Jackson dined regularly at the Cascios' New Jersey home, brought the family to Neverland Ranch, took them on world tours and spent holidays with them. In December 2010, a year and a half after Jackson's death, the parents and three of the siblings appeared on Oprah Winfrey's show to defend Jackson, denying in unison that any "improprieties" had occurred and describing him as a target of false accusations.

According to the lawsuit and a Times interview, that public defense was untrue. The four siblings now participating in the suit say they were groomed from a young age to act as Jackson's "soldiers." Aldo Cascio, now 35, said Jackson began performing oral sex on him when he was about 7, and that the abuse continued for years at Jackson's home, on trips and on tour. He said that days before Jackson's 2009 death, the singer used the code phrase "Disneyland" to request a sexual encounter. Marie Nicole Porte, 37, said she was 12 when Jackson first abused her in her family's home, where he stayed for months after Sept. 11. Dominic, 39, said the abuse began around age 8 during a Euro Disney trip. Eddie, 43, said Jackson first molested him during a 1993 trip to Tel Aviv, taken while Jackson was facing an earlier molestation accusation that resulted in a roughly $23 million settlement in 1994. Frank, the fifth sibling, also says he was abused but is in arbitration with the estate and cannot join the federal suit.

Some siblings recognized the abuse as wrong at the time but felt unable to speak out; others did not fully understand what had happened until watching the 2019 HBO documentary "Leaving Neverland." After the film, Aldo disclosed first, and the others followed.

The Cascios met with estate representatives beginning in 2019 and reached a 2020 settlement under which each plaintiff received approximately $690,000 annually for five years, totaling roughly $16 million across the family. The estate said it agreed "reluctantly" to spare Jackson's family further accusations it considered false. Payments ended in 2025, and negotiations deteriorated. Estate filings say Frank's lawyer demanded an additional $213 million in 2024, threatening a public lawsuit.

Jackson and his estate have consistently denied all molestation allegations. Marty Singer, a lawyer for the estate, characterized the suit as a "desperate money grab" and "forum-shopping" tactic, and described the Cascios as unreliable sources with shifting accounts. The estate plans to seek dismissal or a stay pending arbitration. The lawsuit comes as a Jackson biopic produced with the estate is set to debut, covering only events through 1988.
