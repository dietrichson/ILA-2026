For most of his adult life, Sergey Brin moved through American politics the way many Silicon Valley founders of his generation did: quietly, intermittently, and from the left. The Google co-founder, now 52, was never a fixture on the donor circuit, never a cable-news presence, never a man whose name surfaced in connection with party committees or candidate fund-raisers. When he did engage, his instincts ran in a familiar direction. He helped defend same-sex marriage in California in 2008. He backed Barack Obama's re-election in 2012. He called Donald J. Trump's 2016 victory "deeply offensive" in remarks that leaked from inside Google, and he turned up at an airport protest against the Trump administration's ban on travelers from several Muslim-majority countries. In 2021, with little fanfare, he launched a nonprofit that has since spent at least $88 million on climate and environmental policy, the sort of cause that has long been associated with the politics of the technology industry's first internet generation.

That Sergey Brin is no longer easy to find. According to a report by Theodore Schleifer and Kate Conger published by The New York Times on April 27, 2026, the billionaire has undergone one of the more striking political transformations in recent Silicon Valley memory. He has begun cultivating Republican figures, courting President Trump in person, and spending lavishly to defeat a proposed California tax on the very richest residents of the state. The shift, the Times reported, has unfolded alongside his relationship with Gerelyn Gilbert-Soto, a self-described gut-health influencer in her early 30s who is also a vocal Trump supporter and a familiar presence in the president's social orbit.

The personal dimension of Mr. Brin's evolution is impossible to separate from the political one, and the Times account makes the connection explicit. Ms. Gilbert-Soto, who is 32 and goes by GG, began dating Mr. Brin in 2023, after his divorce from Nicole Shanahan, herself no stranger to political surprise. Ms. Shanahan ran on the 2024 presidential ticket as the running mate of Robert F. Kennedy Jr., an unexpected turn that placed Mr. Brin's previous spouse on a third-party slate that drew attention from across the political spectrum. Ms. Gilbert-Soto's public persona is more openly partisan. She bills herself as a "holistic health coach," has called Mr. Trump her "bestie," and has been photographed with a bedazzled MAGA clutch. She shared a photograph of Mr. Brin, the co-founder of one of the most powerful companies in the world, wearing a red MAGA hat.

Her access to the upper reaches of Trumpworld has been notable. According to the Times, Ms. Gilbert-Soto has visited Mar-a-Lago and the White House, dined with Mr. Trump alongside Mr. Brin and Google's chief executive, Sundar Pichai, and was seated next to Apple's chief executive, Tim Cook, at a White House dinner devoted to artificial intelligence. At that event, Mr. Trump publicly referred to her as Mr. Brin's "MAGA girlfriend," a phrase that captured both the casual intimacy of the encounter and the unusual nature of a Google co-founder's romantic partner being introduced in such terms by the president of the United States. She has also publicly criticized YouTube, the video platform owned by Mr. Brin's own company, over its past suspension of Mr. Trump's account, an awkward overlap of corporate property and personal alliance that underscores how thoroughly the two spheres have begun to bleed into each other.

The political record that has accompanied this personal turn is concrete and traceable in donor filings. The Times reported that Mr. Brin attended a fund-raiser headlined by Vice President JD Vance in May 2025 and donated nearly half a million dollars to the Republican National Committee. At a White House dinner in September, he told Mr. Trump that he was grateful for the administration's support of the technology industry. In March, he was appointed to a White House technology council, an advisory role that places him formally inside the apparatus of an administration he had once denounced. He also contributed to a Republican gubernatorial candidate in California, a state where his company is headquartered and where the political establishment has for decades been reliably Democratic.

The most consequential expression of his new posture, however, is financial, and it concerns a single ballot question. California voters are being asked to consider a one-time 5 percent tax on billionaires in the state, a proposal that has alarmed Mr. Brin enough to inspire what amounts to a small political campaign of his own. Before December 31, the Times reported, he moved his official residence to the Nevada side of Lake Tahoe, while continuing to spend alternating weeks at Google's California headquarters. The maneuver, common enough among the very wealthy when state tax regimes shift, was nonetheless a vivid illustration of the mobility available to those at the top of the income distribution: a change of address that takes a few minutes and an attorney can, if structured carefully, alter a tax liability by tens of millions of dollars.

In a rare public statement, Mr. Brin invoked his family's flight from the Soviet Union in 1979, framing his opposition to the proposed tax in the language of personal history and warning against what he described as socialism taking root in California. The Brin family's emigration story is one of the more familiar biographical facts about him; his father, a mathematician, brought the family to the United States when Sergey was a young child, and the trajectory from Soviet refusenik household to founder of one of the defining American companies of the past quarter century has long been part of his public narrative. The decision to deploy that history in the service of a state tax fight was, in its own way, a marker of how seriously he is taking the measure.

The dollar figures attached to the effort are striking. Mr. Brin has spent $57 million opposing the ballot proposal, the Times reported, channeling money through an entity called Building a Better California and through dark-money vehicles, including a Nevada organization called Compass4 that has raised $93 million from billionaires including Michael Moritz, Eric Schmidt and Chris Larsen. Such vehicles, which obscure the ultimate source of political donations from public view, have become a familiar feature of high-dollar American politics on both sides of the partisan divide. Their presence here is notable mainly because of who is funding them: a slate of figures who built their fortunes in the technology industry and who, only a few years ago, were as likely to appear at a climate summit as at a partisan strategy meeting. Mr. Brin is now California's second-largest individual donor of the cycle, trailing only the gubernatorial candidate Tom Steyer.

The operation is being run, the Times reported, by George Pavlov, who heads Mr. Brin's family office, with behind-the-scenes assistance from the venture capitalist John Doerr. The donor strategy is not strictly partisan in every direction. Mr. Brin has also given to Matt Mahan, a moderate Democrat, and, after Ms. Gilbert-Soto expressed her disapproval of Mr. Mahan, he steered roughly $40,000 to Steve Hilton, a Republican who has been endorsed by Mr. Trump. The episode, brief as it is in the Times account, suggests how deeply the personal and the political have come to overlap in his giving: a relationship that began in 2023 now influences, in small but measurable ways, where his money flows.

The broader context is hard to miss. Mr. Brin's pivot, the Times noted, mirrors a wider rightward drift among Silicon Valley elites who are courting the second Trump administration, a shift that has been one of the more discussed political stories of the past two years. Tech executives who once kept a careful distance from Republican politics, or who openly opposed Mr. Trump during his first term, have in recent months made pilgrimages to Mar-a-Lago, taken seats on White House advisory bodies and recalibrated their public statements. The reasons offered for the shift vary: friction with the regulatory posture of the previous administration, concern about the cost of doing business in deep-blue states, alarm about specific tax proposals like the one Mr. Brin is now spending tens of millions to defeat, and, in some cases, a generational realignment among technology investors whose politics have moved with their portfolios.

Mr. Brin's case nonetheless stands out, both because of the scale of his fortune and because of the distance he has traveled. A man who once described Mr. Trump's election as deeply offensive is now thanking the president for his administration's support of the technology industry. A founder whose nonprofit has poured tens of millions of dollars into climate and environmental policy is also writing the largest checks in California to defeat a tax that the wealthiest residents of the state would otherwise pay. A co-founder of Google has watched his girlfriend criticize Google's own video platform on behalf of a politician with whom he now dines at the White House.

How Mr. Brin himself understands the trajectory is largely a matter of inference. He has spoken rarely in public, even as his checks and his appearances have spoken for him. What is clear from the Times account is that the transformation is real, recent, and expensive, and that the personal and the political, in his case, have become difficult to disentangle. The story of how one of the wealthiest men in the world came to spend $57 million against a single state tax proposal is, in part, the story of a relationship, a residency change and a presidential dinner, and in part the story of a Silicon Valley whose political center of gravity has plainly begun to move.
