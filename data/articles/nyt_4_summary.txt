Google co-founder Sergey Brin, long known for backing liberal causes, has undergone a striking political transformation, embracing Republican figures, courting President Trump, and spending heavily to defeat a proposed California billionaire tax. According to a New York Times report by Theodore Schleifer and Kate Conger published on April 27, 2026, the shift has coincided with Brin's relationship with Gerelyn Gilbert-Soto, a self-described gut-health influencer and outspoken Trump supporter.

Historically, Brin, 52, showed limited political engagement, but when he did weigh in he leaned left. He supported the defense of same-sex marriage in California in 2008, backed Barack Obama's 2012 re-election, called Donald Trump's 2016 victory "deeply offensive" in remarks leaked from Google, and joined a protest against the Trump administration's Muslim ban. In 2021, he quietly launched a nonprofit that has since spent at least 88 million dollars on climate and environmental policy.

That posture has changed. Brin attended a May 2025 fund-raiser headlined by Vice President JD Vance, donated nearly half a million dollars to the Republican National Committee, and told Trump at a White House dinner in September that he was grateful for the administration's support of the tech industry. In March he was appointed to a White House technology council and contributed to a Republican gubernatorial candidate.

Central to this evolution is Gilbert-Soto, 32, who goes by GG. She began dating Brin in 2023 after his divorce from Nicole Shanahan, who ran as Robert F. Kennedy Jr.'s 2024 running mate. Gilbert-Soto styles herself as a "holistic health coach" and Trumpworld personality, calling Trump her "bestie," carrying a bedazzled MAGA clutch, and sharing a photo of Brin wearing a red MAGA hat. She has visited Mar-a-Lago and the White House, dined with Trump alongside Brin and Google CEO Sundar Pichai, and was seated next to Apple's Tim Cook at a White House AI dinner, where Trump publicly referred to her as Brin's "MAGA girlfriend." She has also criticized Google-owned YouTube over its past suspension of Trump's account.

Brin is particularly alarmed by a proposed one-time 5 percent tax on California billionaires. To avoid it, he moved his official residence to the Nevada side of Lake Tahoe before December 31, while continuing to spend alternating weeks at Google's California headquarters. In a rare public statement, he invoked his family's 1979 flight from the Soviet Union, warning against what he called socialism in California. He has spent 57 million dollars opposing the measure, channeling funds through Building a Better California and dark-money vehicles such as Compass4, a Nevada entity that has raised 93 million dollars from billionaires including Michael Moritz, Eric Schmidt, and Chris Larsen. He is now California's second-largest individual donor this cycle, trailing only gubernatorial candidate Tom Steyer.

The operation, run by family-office head George Pavlov with behind-the-scenes help from venture capitalist John Doerr, also includes donations to moderate Democrat Matt Mahan and, after Gilbert-Soto's disapproval of Mahan, roughly 40,000 dollars to Trump-endorsed Republican Steve Hilton. Brin's pivot mirrors a wider rightward drift among Silicon Valley elites courting the second Trump administration.
